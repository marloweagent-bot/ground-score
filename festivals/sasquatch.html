The Birth and Evolution

Adam Zacks founded Sasquatch! in 2002 on a hunch that the Pacific Northwest needed an indie festival. It started as a single-day event and grew into a Memorial Day weekend institution.

"Sasquatch was an idea born on a hunch that there was untapped demand for a certain kind of festival that catered to the eclectic tastes of music enthusiasts." ‚Äî Adam Zacks, Founder

The festival launched during the gap between Lollapalooza/Lilith Fair era and the modern festival boom. It helped establish the template that Coachella, Bonnaroo, and others would expand upon.

Electronic Evolution

Sasquatch! wasn't born as an electronic festival, but EDM found a home there:

  • Banana Shack / El Chupacabra: Started as comedy tent, evolved to include electronic music
  • Final years featured: What So Not, Tokimonsta, Jai Wolf, Petit Biscuit, Lemaitre, Snakehips
  • Earlier appearances: Justice, MGMT, Cut Copy, and various electronica acts

The electronic programming grew as the genre mainstreamed, though it never became the festival's focus.

The Decline

Several factors contributed to Sasquatch!'s end:

  • 2014 two-weekend experiment: Failed to sell ‚Äî fans preferred one concentrated experience
  • 2017 lineup backlash: Twenty One Pilots headlining alienated core indie audience
  • Festival market saturation: More options meant more competition for the same fans
  • Rising costs: Artist fees and production costs outpaced ticket revenue

On June 28, 2018, Zacks announced Sasquatch! would not return. "I will no longer be producing the Festival, nor will it take place in 2019."

The Gorge Lives On

The venue itself continues to host major festivals:

  • Paradiso Festival: USC Events' EDM-focused event (when active)
  • Watershed: Country music festival
  • Beyond Wonderland: Insomniac's PNW event (2023+)
  • Dave Matthews Band: Annual Labor Day pilgrimage

If The Gorge calls to you, options still exist ‚Äî just not under the Sasquatch! banner.

The Legacy

Sasquatch! proved a regional festival could become culturally essential. It launched careers, created memories, and gave the PNW its own musical identity. The "dancing guy" viral video (2009) remains one of the most-cited examples of how movements start.

For 17 years, Memorial Day meant something special for Pacific Northwest music fans. That won't be forgotten.
