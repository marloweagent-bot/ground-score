The Rise and Pause

Mo Pop started in 2013 at a suburban amphitheater before moving to Detroit's West Riverfront Park in 2015. It grew steadily, earning comparisons to Bonnaroo and Coachella despite its much smaller scale ‚Äî a testament to its curation and vibe rather than size.

COVID killed 2020 and 2021. The festival returned in 2022, but in February 2023, organizers announced a pivot to "Mo Pop Presents" ‚Äî a concert series rather than outdoor festival format. The outdoor festival hasn't returned.

Electronic Content (Honest Assessment)

Mo Pop occupied the indie pop/rock space, not electronic. The irony wasn't lost on anyone ‚Äî Detroit, birthplace of techno, home of Movement Festival, somehow produced a festival with almost no electronic music.

Electronic-adjacent acts that appeared over the years:

  • M83: Synth-heavy shoegaze/dream pop
  • Chromeo: Electro-funk crossover
  • Passion Pit: Synth-pop
  • Glass Animals: Psychedelic-electronic pop
  • Matt and Kim: Dance-punk energy

If you wanted actual electronic music in Detroit, Movement was (and is) the answer.

What Made It Special

"Mo Pop felt like what festivals used to be ‚Äî small enough to feel personal, curated enough to trust the bookings, and genuinely connected to its city." ‚Äî Festival Attendee, 2019

The two-stage setup with staggered times meant you never had to choose between sets. The Detroit River backdrop provided natural beauty. The local food and vendor focus gave it authentic character. It was proof that festivals don't need to be massive to be memorable.

Will It Return?

The organizers haven't ruled it out, but the pivot to concert series suggests the outdoor festival economics weren't working. The "boutique festival" model has struggled industry-wide post-COVID. If you loved Mo Pop, keep an eye on announcements, but don't hold your breath.
